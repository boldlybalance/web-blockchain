SEC and CFTC Issue Landmark Crypto Classification Framework

For the first time, US regulators have jointly classified crypto assets. The verdict: most crypto is not a security. Here's what this means for the industry.

March 29, 2026

A Historic Shift

March 17, 2026 marked a turning point for US crypto regulation. The SEC and CFTC issued a joint interpretive guidance—the first framework of its kind—classifying crypto assets into five distinct categories.

The headline: "most crypto assets are not securities" under US law. This isn't just rhetoric—it's the clearest regulatory signal the industry has ever received from Washington.

What Changed

The Five-Category Framework

Rather than treating all crypto assets identically, the framework distinguishes between:

  • Payment tokens (BTC, stablecoins)—treated as commodities
  • Utility tokens—access rights, not investment contracts
  • Governance tokens—decentralized protocol participation
  • Asset-backed tokens—structured products (REIT-style)
  • Securities tokens—investment contracts meeting Howey test

This matters because it means decentralized protocols like Uniswap, Aave, and Ethereum nodes can operate without securities registration—provided governance remains sufficiently decentralized.

Exchanges Breathe Easier

US crypto exchanges have operated under a cloud of regulatory uncertainty for years. Coinbase, Kraken, and others faced SEC enforcement actions claiming tokens were unregistered securities. The new framework gives them a defensible position: if the token isn't a security under the five-category test, the exchange isn't violating securities law by listing it.

The Bigger Picture

This alignment between SEC and CFTC is notable. The two agencies have historically turf-warred over crypto jurisdiction. The fact they produced joint guidance suggests coordination at the political level—a signal that the administration is serious about crypto-friendly policy.

Industry groups had pushed for this clarity for nearly a decade. The cryptocurrency industry has lobbied extensively, with trade associations spending millions on advocacy. Institutional players—including BlackRock, Fidelity, and Franklin Templeton—had also pressured regulators to provide clearer rules for the ETF products they'd built.

What's Still Unclear

No framework resolves everything. Key remaining questions:

  • Decentralization threshold—how "decentralized" must a protocol be to qualify for the governance token exemption?
  • NFTs and digital collectibles—largely unaddressed
  • DeFi protocol liability—if a protocol is hacked or rug-pulled, does the classification shield developers?

The Bottom Line

Regulatory clarity is here—mostly. The SEC/CFTC framework doesn't legalize everything (securities tokens still exist), but it draws bright lines that let builders and investors plan with confidence.

For the first time, you can roughly know where you stand before building a product, launching a token, or listing an asset on a US exchange. That's progress.
